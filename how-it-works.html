How Analyst Assist works

Analyst Assist turns opponent film data and live game charting into a fast tendency board. It does not claim to know the next play with certainty. It ranks the most likely offensive answers based on situation, opponent history, live adjustments, sequence behavior, and star-player impact.

Coach workflow

1

Load opponent data

Import charted film or manually enter plays with down, distance, field zone, personnel, formation, concept, play type, result, and optional star-player status.

2

Select the current situation

During prep or live game mode, choose down, distance, zone, personnel, formation family, hash, tempo, and star status.

3

The engine scores every active concept

Each possible call gets a weighted score from matching film, matching live-game plays, general baseline tendency, sequence patterns, and star-player adjustments.

4

Scores become percentages

The system normalizes all concept scores so the board can display top plays plus overall Run, Pass, RPO, and Screen probability.

5

Coaches log what actually happened

The box coach can log full detail. The sideline coach simply taps Run %, Pass %, RPO %, or Screen % to record accountability and keep the board synced.

What the prediction is

A weighted tendency model. It is best used as a coach alert system: "what should we be ready for right now?"

Best use: identify tendency, alert the sideline, and compare film tendency vs. live tendency.

Low sample sizes should be treated as alerts, not certainties.

How concepts are scored

For each possible offensive concept, the engine blends multiple layers of information into a single score.

Film data How often the opponent has run this concept in similar situations across your scouting film. The more closely the film situation matches the current game situation, the more weight it carries.
Live game data What the opponent has actually run so far in this game. Live data becomes more influential as the game goes on and a larger in-game sample builds up.
Baseline football logic General situational tendencies — for example, most offenses pass more on 3rd and long. This backstops the model when data is limited.
Sequence and star adjustments Recent play sequence patterns and star-player on/off tendencies can shift the final score up or down for specific concepts.

How situation matching works

Not all film plays are equally relevant. The engine gives the most credit to plays that closely match the current situation, then considers broader tendency buckets as backup.

Exact situation match Plays with the same down, distance bucket, and field zone as right now. These carry the most weight.
Down and distance match Plays with the same down and distance category, regardless of field position.
Personnel and formation match Plays run from the same personnel grouping or formation family.
Game phase and overall rate Broader patterns like red zone, two-minute, or backed-up sequences, plus the opponent's general tendency across the entire dataset.

How percentages are calculated

After every concept is scored, the scores are converted to percentages by comparing each concept's score against the total score of all active concepts combined. The board then groups those percentages into Run, Pass, RPO, and Screen splits.

The percentages reflect relative likelihood — a concept at 40% is roughly twice as likely as one at 20%, based on the available data.

Live-game weighting

Live game data becomes more important as more plays are logged. Early in the game, film study still carries most of the model. As the opponent reveals its plan, live tendency gets more influence.

Early game Film data drives most of the prediction. The model has not yet seen enough live plays to shift significantly.
Mid and late game Live tendency carries progressively more weight. If the opponent is running something unexpected, the board will reflect it faster.
You can also manually set the live weight in Prep Center if you want to trust film more or lean on live data earlier.

Sequence awareness

The engine looks at what the opponent tends to call after the last logged concept or play type. If an offense consistently follows a certain play with a specific call, that pattern gets factored into the current prediction.

Sequence patterns only influence the score meaningfully once enough play-after-play samples exist. Early in a game, they carry limited weight.

Star-player adjustment

If you chart whether a key offensive player is on or off the field, the engine adjusts concept likelihood based on how that player's presence has historically changed the offense's behavior.

Star ON field Concepts that skew toward that player's usage — designed touches, target concepts, red zone involvement — get a positive adjustment.
Star OFF field Those same concept adjustments are reversed, reflecting that the offense may lean on different personnel or play types.

If there are not enough ON/OFF samples, the system shows a low-confidence alert instead of forcing a strong adjustment.

Sideline mode

Sideline mode is intentionally simpler: see the split, see the alert, tap the actual play type, and keep moving. No setup required during the game.

How to log a play on the sideline Tap Run %, Pass %, RPO %, or Screen % after the play to record what the offense actually did. The board stays synced with the box automatically.
